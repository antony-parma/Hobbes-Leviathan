“What Would Hobbes Do?” — Classic & Contemporary Scenarios

12 scenario-based multiple choice questions. Read the setup, pick the answer, then reveal explanations or grade all at once.

Classic Hobbes (17th‑century themes)

Scenario 1
Civil Strife in a Kingdom

After the sudden death of a king, a medieval kingdom is thrown into disarray. Nobles compete for influence, peasants rise in protest over food shortages, and rival militias begin patrolling towns. Merchants stop trading because roads are unsafe, and rumors spread that foreign armies may invade while the kingdom is divided. Several councils propose temporary power-sharing, but disagreements over succession keep dissolving into shouting matches and threats of violence. Ordinary citizens long for peace but fear whichever faction seizes control might rule tyrannically. Some argue it is better to wait for the “natural balance” of power to emerge; others believe a strong authority must be established immediately, even if it is harsh.

Question: According to Hobbes, what is the best course of action in this crisis?
Hobbes holds that civil war is the greatest evil; only a unified, absolute sovereign can end the “war of all against all” and secure peace. Hence (B).
Scenario 2
A New Colony Without Laws

A group of settlers establishes a colony far from their homeland. At first, cooperation is strong: people share food, build shelters together, and defend against wild animals. But as resources become scarce, disputes break out. Some claim ownership of fertile plots, others demand more than their fair share of harvest, and violent quarrels erupt. There is no recognized leader or legal framework to settle conflicts. Small groups form around charismatic figures, each threatening others to secure their interests. Trust begins to vanish, and trade collapses because agreements are frequently broken. The settlers face a choice: either continue in a fragile state of disorder, or create a governing authority that can command obedience and guarantee safety.

Question: What would Hobbes argue the settlers must do?
Hobbes’s social contract authorizes a sovereign with coercive power to end the state of nature’s insecurity. Answer (A).
Scenario 3
Freedom of Speech in a Restless Republic

In a republic recovering from war, citizens fiercely debate policies in public forums. Some criticize the ruling council for corruption, others spread conspiracy theories about traitors, and a few advocate rebellion. Pamphlets circulate denouncing leaders and urging the population to resist taxation. Though much of this speech is harmless, outbreaks of mob violence follow certain fiery orators. Council members are torn: some wish to guarantee freedom of expression as a safeguard against tyranny, while others argue that such speech is destabilizing and must be silenced. Meanwhile, ordinary people fear both oppression and civil war, unsure which poses the greater threat.

Question: What would Hobbes recommend regarding speech in this republic?
For Hobbes, doctrines subversive of obedience threaten peace; the sovereign may regulate speech to prevent disorder. (B).
Scenario 4
Religious Division in a State

A prosperous state is suddenly divided by religious conflict. Two sects, once tolerant of each other, begin to clash violently in the streets. Each side accuses the other of heresy and corruption, and priests urge followers to resist laws they see as contrary to divine command. The sovereign, though politically strong, is hesitant to interfere with religious practices for fear of alienating half the population. However, as fights spread, trade falters, and once-safe neighborhoods turn into battlegrounds. The state risks collapse unless a decisive policy is adopted. Advisors debate whether religious authority should operate independently or under the control of the ruler.

Question: How would Hobbes view the solution to religious strife?
Hobbes is Erastian: ecclesiastical power must be subordinate to the civil sovereign to prevent sectarian war. (B).
Scenario 5
A Rebellion Against Heavy Taxes

A sovereign imposes high taxes to fund defense against an aggressive neighbor. Citizens grumble, but the ruler insists survival requires sacrifice. Soon, rebels gather, claiming the sovereign is unjust and tyrannical. They burn tax records and refuse payments. The sovereign responds with threats of punishment, but the rebellion grows. Merchants are divided: some want order restored quickly, others secretly fund rebels in hopes of regime change. Violence spreads to towns, and it becomes unclear whether the sovereign can maintain authority without resorting to extreme brutality.

Question: What would Hobbes say about rebellion in this context?
Except in cases of immediate self‑preservation (e.g., being commanded to die), Hobbes denies a right to rebel; stability requires obedience. (A).
Scenario 6
The Question of International Order

Two powerful states border each other uneasily. Though they trade, each fears betrayal and keeps armies ready at the frontier. Spies and propaganda campaigns sow suspicion, and border skirmishes occasionally erupt. Citizens on both sides live in constant anxiety, and leaders justify military buildup as “necessary defense.” Some philosophers in these states argue that an international authority should govern disputes and guarantee peace. Others dismiss the idea, claiming that no state would surrender its sovereignty to an external power. The risk of full-scale war looms large.

Question: How would Hobbes likely interpret relations between states?
For Hobbes, without a supranational sovereign states face each other as individuals do in nature—under insecurity. (A).

Modern / Contemporary Hobbes

Scenario 7
Pandemic Governance and Misinformation

A fast-moving respiratory virus overwhelms hospitals. Early measures—masking, travel limits, targeted closures—reduce spread but spark backlash. Influencers sell miracle cures, neighborhood groups block contact tracers, and a few local mayors refuse to enforce rules, claiming “medical freedom.” Supply chains buckle as truckers protest vaccination mandates, while vulnerable communities face the highest mortality. A national task force proposes a unified emergency code: standardized data reporting, compulsory quarantine for confirmed cases, and penalties for spreading demonstrably false medical directives. Civil libertarians warn of “pandemic authoritarianism,” arguing local experimentation yields better results. Doctors counter that patchwork policies prolong the crisis and erode trust. Meanwhile, a new variant emerges.

Question: What would Hobbes advocate in this situation?
Hobbes subordinates liberty to peace and safety in emergencies; centralized, uniform commands curb disorder and fear. (A).
Scenario 8
Social Media Platforms and Public Order

A major platform becomes the primary space for political discourse. During a tense election season, coordinated networks flood it with deepfakes, doxxing, and calls to “defend polling stations” with force. The company claims neutrality but monetizes engagement spikes. Police insist the platform’s choices directly affect public safety and request emergency throttling of incitement and false voting instructions. Legislators consider a statute designating dominant platforms as “public order utilities” subject to binding safety standards, including identity verification for political ads and rapid takedowns. Critics fear speech control by either corporations or the state; activists argue that chaotic discourse threatens the peace.

Question: What would Hobbes recommend?
Where speech incites disorder, Hobbes empowers the civil sovereign to regulate it as matter of public peace. (A).
Scenario 9
AI and Autonomous Urban Mobility

A city authorizes autonomous vehicles (AVs) to reduce accidents. Early gains are real, but edge-case crashes spark outrage. Different AV firms follow incompatible safety rules; emergency responders complain that vehicles block fire lanes. Protesters demand a moratorium; disability advocates warn that suspending AVs removes vital mobility. A consortium proposes a single binding “City Driving Code” enforced by a public regulator: mandatory black-box logs, standardized hazard protocols, and remote shutdown authority during incidents. Companies argue innovation will stall. Residents just want streets to be predictably safe.

Question: What would Hobbes likely support?
Hobbes values uniform, enforceable rules that remove uncertainty; fragmented standards reintroduce danger. (A).
Scenario 10
Climate Displacement and City Security

After successive heat waves and floods, hundreds of thousands migrate toward cooler, higher cities. Informal camps grow along highways; vigilante groups form to “protect neighborhoods.” Property insurers exit the region, private security patrols sell protection, and fights erupt over water distribution. City hall drafts an emergency integration plan: temporary housing zones, rationing under a single command, compulsory registration, and stiff penalties for private militias. Civil society organizations demand community-led councils instead, fearing abuses by a centralized command. Business leaders argue only a strong, predictable authority can keep supply chains and hospitals functioning.

Question: What would Hobbes prioritize?
Preventing descent into factional violence is paramount; centralized command protects life and goods. (A).
Scenario 11
Critical Infrastructure Cyberattack

A coordinated cyberattack cripples the power grid and payment systems. Supermarkets limit cash sales; rumors trigger runs on fuel. Competing agencies release contradictory guidance; some governors threaten to commandeer data centers “for public safety.” A national directive is proposed: one incident commander with legal power to compel private network operators, mandate patching, and restrict public disclosures during active breaches. Tech companies warn about overreach and liability; privacy groups fear secrecy will hide mistakes. Emergency rooms report rising violence as neighborhoods go dark.

Question: What would Hobbes likely counsel?
Security of the commonwealth requires a single, uncontested commander to quell panic and coordinate defense. (A).
Scenario 12
Biotech DIY and Public Safety

Affordable gene-editing kits let hobbyists culture microbes at home. Most are harmless education projects, but a few online forums begin sharing protocols for immune-evasive variants. Couriers deliver enzymes and reagents overnight; enforcement is patchy. Universities propose a licensing regime: mandatory training, facility inspections, and penalties for unauthorized experimentation; shipment controls would route all sensitive materials through a monitored chain. DIY enthusiasts argue such rules crush innovation and democratized science. Public health officials warn that even a single reckless experiment could spiral.

Question: From a Hobbesian lens, what policy fits best?
Hobbes privileges safety over unfettered liberty; binding licensing with inspections best averts catastrophic risk. (A).
